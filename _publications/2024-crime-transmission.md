**Abstract**

This paper examines the intergenerational transmission of criminal behavior in the context of a developing economy. Using a novel administrative dataset covering the full cohort of children born in Chile in 2010, I estimate the conditional association between parental contact with the justice system and adolescent offending through a Linear Probability Model with school and commune fixed effects.

The results show that parental criminal exposure is a robust predictor of adolescent offending, but that this association varies substantially across family and offense characteristics. I document four dimensions of heterogeneity. First, *severity matters*: adolescent offending risk increases non-linearly with parental offense severity, and parental incarceration is associated with offending rates approximately 500% higher than the control mean. Second, *timing matters*: the strongest associations are observed when exposure occurs during the transition to adolescence, ages 11–14, consistent with a proximal-risk pattern. Third, the association differs by *gender and parental structure*: boys face elevated risk from both maternal and paternal exposure independently, whereas girls' risk increases most clearly in dual-parent criminal households. Fourth, *offense type matters*: domestic violence and drug-related offenses are among the most strongly associated forms of parental exposure, with risk increases of approximately 367% and 600% relative to the control mean, respectively.

These findings suggest that intergenerational crime is not a homogeneous process. Rather, the association between parental and adolescent criminal involvement is concentrated among specific high-risk family profiles, particularly those involving severe, recent, or dual-parent exposure.
